There are two dominant metaphors for a personal knowledge system: the **vault** and the **garden**.

The vault is where you keep things safe. It's complete, organized, indexed. You put things in, you retrieve them, nothing changes. A vault is a storage unit. Its job is fidelity — what you put in is exactly what you get out.

The garden is where things grow. It's partial, messy, seasonal. You plant things, they change, some die, some cross-pollinate. A garden is a living system. Its job is transformation — what you put in is not what you get out.

> [!abstract] The two systems
> - **Vault**: archive, reference, source of truth. Search-oriented. Complete.
> - **Garden**: working memory, thinking tool, draft space. Browse-oriented. Partial.

Most people need both. The vault holds your receipts, your contracts, your reference material — the things you need to find exactly as they were. The garden holds your thinking — the things you're working on, the connections you're testing, the ideas that aren't finished yet.

The mistake is confusing them. If you treat your garden like a vault — if every note has to be polished, complete, correctly filed — you'll never plant anything. The garden's value comes from its messiness, its half-formed thoughts, its links that don't quite make sense yet.

> [!warning]
> Don't optimize your garden for retrieval before you've optimized it for growth. A clean, empty garden is just a vault with worse lighting.

[[Bikeshedding and the Garden|Bikeshedding]] is what happens when you treat the garden like a vault — spending all your time organizing instead of growing. The [[Compounding Knowledge|compounding]] effect only works when the notes are allowed to be imperfect, to sit next to each other, to cross-pollinate.

The vault is for the past. The garden is for the future. Keep them separate.
