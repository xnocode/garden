There's a difference between reading and finishing. Most of what we call reading is actually finishing — moving our eyes across pages until we reach the end, so we can say we did it. The information enters short-term memory, gets summarized into a sentence, and evaporates.

Slow reading is the opposite. You read a paragraph, and then you stop. You think about it. You write down what it made you feel. You follow a link to something it reminds you of. You come back the next day and read the same paragraph again, and it means something different because you're different.

> [!quote]
> "The reader should be the writer's co-conspirator. The text is just the meeting place." — adapted from Italo Calvino

A digital garden is built for slow reading. ==There is no finish line.== No progress bar (well, there is one, but it's not the point). No "you've read 73% of this note." You read until you're done — which might be halfway through, or might be three paragraphs in, or might be the whole thing twice.

> [!tip] Three habits of slow reading
> 1. **Stop when something catches you.** Don't push through to the end. If a sentence makes you think, stop and think.
> 2. **Write while you read.** A note in the margin, a link to another note, a question. Reading and writing are not separate activities.
> 3. **Come back.** A good note rewards re-reading. You'll be a different person next time.

This is why the [[The Map Is Not the Territory|map-is-not-territory]] principle matters. If you're only scanning titles and snippets — only reading the map — you're not slow reading. You're finishing. The territory is slower and more valuable.
