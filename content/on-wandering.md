There's a difference between searching and wandering. Searching has a target — you know what you're looking for, and you stop when you find it. Wandering has no target. You move, you notice, you follow a thread, and you stop when something catches you.

The internet is very good at searching. It is almost hostile to wandering. Feeds, recommendations, and search results all push you toward the thing you already wanted — or the thing the algorithm already knows about. The surprise, the detour, the strange connection between two ideas you'd never have put together — that's been designed out of most interfaces.

> [!quote]
> The garden is not a filing cabinet. It's a place you walk through.

A digital garden, at its best, is built for wandering. The wikilinks are invitations, not directions. The backlinks show you who else has been here. The graph is a map of paths you might take — not a list of tasks to complete. You don't read a garden cover to cover. You wander until you find the thing you didn't know you were looking for.

This is why ==the note is the unit, not the post==. A post is a destination. A note is a place. You can return to a place. You can pass through it on the way to somewhere else. You can find it again years later and notice something you missed.

The cost of wandering is inefficiency. You'll read notes that don't help you. You'll follow links to dead ends. You'll spend twenty minutes on a tangent. But the payoff — the unexpected connection, the idea that reorganizes everything — is worth the waste. ==Efficiency is for factories. Gardens are for growing.==

So: don't read this note and leave. Click a link. Follow a backlink. Open the graph and pick a node at random. Get lost for a few minutes. That's the whole point.
