A digital garden is a collection of notes that you tend rather than publish. The metaphor is doing real work here: a blog is a stream of finished posts flowing past, dated and discarded, while a garden is a place you return to, weed, prune, and let cross-pollinate. Both are valid; they serve different needs.

## The stream vs. the garden

A blog post is a *product*. You research, draft, edit, publish, and move on. The post freezes a conclusion in time. A garden note is a *process*. It can sit as a seedling for months, then get revised when something you learn later reshapes it. Notes link to each other instead of being ordered by date, so the structure is spatial rather than chronological.

> [!quote] Maggie Appleton
> "A digital garden is an open collection of notes... the emphasis is on growth, connection, and exploration rather than presentation."

That last word — *presentation* — is the crux. Blogs optimize for being read; gardens optimize for being *thought in*. The audience for a garden is often the gardener themselves, with readers welcome to overhear.

## Why cultivate instead of publish?

Publishing asks "is this done?" Cultivating asks "is this growing?" The second question is friendlier. It lets me publish half-formed ideas without the pressure of a polished takeaway, then return to them when I have more to say. Notes accumulate *value* over time rather than losing it the way a dated post does.

This also changes how I read my own past. In a blog, old posts embarrass me — they're snapshots of who I was. In a garden, old notes are compost. I revise them in place, and the revision history (or lack thereof) becomes part of the texture.
