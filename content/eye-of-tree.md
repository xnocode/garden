There are times when I feel that my father can see through me more clearly than I can see myself. He understands what I am capable of, even when I doubt it. Whenever I listen to his advice, my life seems to move in the right direction. When I ignore it, everything quickly becomes difficult, as if I lose my way. 

He once told me, "Right now, I am here to give you shade when you need it. Use this time to build yourself. One day I will be gone. What will you do then? You must learn how to build your own road and how to protect it." He reminds me not to miss a single salah and to go to the mosque for every prayer. He tells me to eat properly, to keep my room clean, and to take care of myself. At the same time, he gives me freedom and trusts me to make my own choices.

Sometimes I feel like I do not deserve this kind of family. They are supportive, patient, and kind. If I truly cared for them as much as I claim to, would I have pushed myself harder? Did I really give my best effort? The honest answer is no. Many times, I did not. Without their support, I often feel powerless. I have not secured a place or position that proves I can stand entirely on my own. Even the things I achieved in the past slipped away from me. Perhaps I did not protect them with enough discipline, gratitude, and commitment.

My grandfather was a great man. I hope that one day I can become someone like him. People say that I have his kindness, and I take comfort in that. But I do not yet possess his capability, strength, or courage. He was a legend. He did not wait for opportunities to appear—he created them. He built his own kingdom through effort and determination. He did not depend on others to shape his destiny. Even today, he remains an inspiration to those who knew him.

When I compare myself to him, I feel small. I do not have a kingdom to protect. Instead, it often feels as though my own kingdom is constantly burning, consumed by fires of regret, fear, and wasted potential. But perhaps this realization is not meant to destroy me. Perhaps it is a warning and an invitation. My father's shade will not last forever. The legacy of my grandfather is not something to admire from a distance; it is a standard to learn from.

I still have time. I can pray with sincerity. I can discipline myself in small things. I can clean my room, nourish my body, honor my family, and build my road one stone at a time. I can learn to protect what I build. I can become reliable before life demands it from me.

I may not be the man my grandfather was today, but I do not have to remain the man I am now. 

The kingdom I need to build is my own.
