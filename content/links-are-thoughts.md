A footnote says "see also." A link says "this is related to that." But a wikilink in a digital garden says something stronger: ==this is part of that.==

When you write a note and link it to another note, you're making a claim. You're saying: these two things belong together. The link is the thought — the connection itself is the idea, not just a way of navigating between ideas.

This is why the [[Wikilinks and Backlinks|backlinks]] panel matters. It's not just "who links to me." It's "who else thought I was part of their argument." Every backlink is someone else's thought about how your note fits into theirs.

> [!example]
> Consider the difference:
> - **Footnote**: "For more on Zettelkasten, see Luhmann (1981)." → a pointer.
> - **Wikilink**: "The [[Zettelkasten Method]] works because..." → a claim that this note is part of the Zettelkasten story.
>
> The wikilink is doing more work. It's not just sending you somewhere; it's saying the current note doesn't make complete sense without that other one.

This is also why [[Tags and Organization|tags]] are weaker than links. A tag says "this note is about X." A link says "this note is related to that specific other note." Tags are categories; links are arguments. Categories sort. Arguments connect.

> [!tip]
> When you write a note, count the links. If there are zero, ask: is this note connected to anything I've already written? If not, is it connected to anything I will write? A note with no links is an island — and islands are hard to reach.

The [[Knowledge graph|graph]] is the sum of all these thoughts. Every edge is a claim someone made about how two ideas relate. The graph isn't a map of the notes — it's a map of the thinking.
