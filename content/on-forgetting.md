We treat forgetting as a failure. If you read a book and can't remember it a year later, you say you've forgotten it — as if the reading was wasted. But that's not how memory works, and it's not how a garden works either.

> [!quote]
> "Memory is not a recording. It's a reconstruction. What you keep is not what happened — it's what was useful enough to rebuild."

The notes you don't remember writing are not lost. They're compost. They broke down into the structure of your thinking — into the way you frame problems, the examples you reach for, the connections you make. ==You don't need to remember a note for it to have changed you.==

This is why a digital garden is better than a notebook for long-term thinking. A notebook demands you remember where you wrote something. A garden lets the links remember for you. The [[Backlinks|backlinks]] panel is a prosthetic memory — it shows you who else thought this note mattered, even if you've forgotten.

> [!abstract] Three kinds of forgetting
> 1. **Composting** — the note breaks down into your general thinking. Good.
> 2. **Archiving** — the note is dormant but findable. Fine.
> 3. **Losing** — the note is gone and unfindable. The only bad kind.

The garden is designed to prevent the third kind. As long as a note is in the vault with `draft: false`, it's findable — by search, by tag, by graph, by backlink. You don't have to remember it. You just have to be able to encounter it again.

The [[On Reading Slowly|slow reading]] philosophy applies here too. You read a note, you forget the details, but the next time you encounter the idea, you recognize it. That recognition is the residue. That's what the garden is growing.
