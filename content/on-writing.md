Writing is the thing I keep doing despite having nothing clever to say about how it works. Every essay I've started about writing has collapsed into either cliché ("just write every day") or pretension ("the blank page is a mirror for the soul"), and I'm not sure this one will escape that gravity. But the garden is the right place to try, because here an unfinished essay is allowed to sit until it's ready.

## The resistance is the material

The hardest part of writing isn't the writing — it's the moment just before, when the page is empty and the thought feels too large or too small to commit to a sentence. I've spent more hours avoiding writing than writing. The trick I've landed on, after years of fighting it, is to treat the resistance itself as information. If a sentence won't come, the thought usually isn't ready, and forcing it produces prose I'll have to delete. Better to write a bad sentence honestly than a polished one I don't believe.

## Revision is where the writing is

First drafts are for figuring out what I think. Revision is for figuring out what I *meant*. I used to conflate these — I'd try to write a clean first draft and end up blocked, because the part of me that explores and the part of me that polishes cannot both be in the room at once. Now I let the first draft be a mess and trust revision to find the shape. Most of what I like in my own writing was discovered in revision, not invented in the draft.

## The garden lowers the stakes

A blog post asks to be finished. A garden note asks to be *started*. That difference is enormous for someone like me who freezes under "finished." In the garden, I can publish a fragment and revise it for years — see [[Digital Gardens]] for why this isn't laziness but a different relationship to completion. The pressure comes off, and paradoxically I write more, because the bar isn't "is this done?" but "is this honest?"

## A small discipline

Write the sentence you actually believe, not the sentence that sounds like the kind of thing you'd say. Read it aloud. If you stumble, the reader will too — fix it now, not later. Then publish it into the garden and let it be wrong in public, which is the only way it has a chance to become right.
