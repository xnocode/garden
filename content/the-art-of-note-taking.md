Most note-taking advice is about *tools* — which app, which folder structure, which shortcut. That's the easy part. The harder and more interesting question is what makes a note *good*, in the sense of aging well: a note you'll still be glad you wrote in three years. ==A good note is one your future self can pick up cold and use.==

%% Re-read this essay quarterly. The thesis keeps drifting toward "tools" whenever I'm not paying attention. %%

## Write for a forgetful reader

Assume the reader — usually future-you — remembers nothing about the context. That means stating the claim up front, defining jargon the first time it appears, and linking to the source. The [[Zettelkasten Method]] calls this the principle of self-containment, and it's the single highest-leverage habit I know. A note that says "see also Tuesday's lecture" is useless by Friday.

## Take a position

A neutral summary is a dead note. "Smith argues that markets are efficient" is forgettable; "Smith is right about prices but wrong about information, and here's why" is a note that will keep generating essays. This is the difference between a filing cabinet and a thinking tool — the cabinet stores, the tool *thinks*. Position-taking is uncomfortable because it commits you, but that's exactly what makes a note alive.

## Revise without mercy

Notes are not immutable. When you learn something that contradicts a note, edit the note — don't write a new one next to it. A garden where every revision becomes a new plant is just a stream by another name. I keep a small "last revised" line at the top of notes I change often, so I can see how my mind has moved.

## Link before you file

If you remember only one rule: before you decide *where* a note goes, decide *what it connects to.[^1]* Links are the filing system in a garden. A note with three good links is findable from three directions; a note with none is findable only if you remember its title, which you won't.

## A note is a promise

Every note is implicitly a promise to your future self that this was worth holding onto. The ones that turn out not to be? Delete them freely. Compost is only valuable if it's allowed to rot.
