I think I've slowly lost my ability to listen. Not in the sense that I can't hear people anymore, but in the sense that words no longer seem to reach the place where they are supposed to matter. Advice reaches my ears but rarely reaches my actions. Reminders reach my mind but rarely reach my habits. I can sit through an entire lecture, read an entire chapter, listen to an entire reminder, and still remain the same person afterward. Sometimes I wonder when that started happening. Maybe it happened gradually, so gradually that I never noticed it. Maybe every ignored reminder took something away from me until one day I realized I could still hear everything, yet nothing was truly changing me.

The strange thing is that I know better. I know what I should be doing. I know what I should avoid. I know which habits are helping me and which ones are destroying me. I know that life is temporary. I know that every day that passes is a day I will never get back. I know that death is certain and that my time is limited. Yet somehow knowledge has become separated from action. It is as though there are two versions of me. One version understands everything, and the other version continues making the same mistakes despite understanding them.

Perhaps that is why I think about Jahannam more than I used to. Not because I enjoy thinking about punishment, but because I find myself wondering how people end up there in the first place. The Quran tells us that Jahannam has seven gates and that every gate has its assigned people. Yet what frightens me is not the number of gates. What frightens me is the realization that people do not suddenly arrive there. No one wakes up one morning and decides to throw away their soul. The road toward destruction is usually much quieter than that. It begins with a small compromise, a small excuse, a small delay, a small act of negligence that seems harmless at the time.

I imagine that very few people ever looked at their lives and thought, "I want to become one of the people of Jahannam." Instead, they probably convinced themselves they still had time. Time to repent. Time to improve. Time to pray more consistently. Time to leave behind bad habits. Time to become serious about faith. Time to become better. And when I think about that, I become uncomfortable because I recognize that same mindset in myself. How many times have I told myself that I will change tomorrow? How many times have I delayed something important because I assumed another opportunity would come? How many times have I replaced action with intention?

The truth is that intentions are cheap. I have collected enough good intentions to fill an entire lifetime. I have intended to become more disciplined. I have intended to become more grateful. I have intended to become more consistent in worship. I have intended to control my anger, improve my character, and spend my time more wisely. Yet intention without action slowly becomes a form of self-deception. At some point, a person has to stop planning to change and actually change.

Sometimes I wonder whether the greatest danger is not ignorance but familiarity. When you hear something often enough, you stop feeling its weight. You hear verses about death so many times that death begins to feel distant. You hear reminders about accountability so many times that accountability begins to feel theoretical. You hear warnings about Jahannam so many times that they become words instead of realities. Maybe that is what happened to me. Maybe I became too familiar with reminders. Maybe I learned how to agree with them without allowing them to transform me.

When I look back at my life, I can see countless moments that should have changed me. Moments when I felt genuine regret. Moments when I promised myself I would become better. Moments when I was certain that this time would be different. Yet somehow the feeling faded, life continued, distractions returned, and I went back to being the same person. It is difficult to admit that. Nobody likes admitting that they keep losing the same battle. Nobody likes admitting that they have become experts at restarting without progressing.

And perhaps that is what truly scares me. Not that I make mistakes, because every human being makes mistakes. Not that I fall short, because every human being falls short. What scares me is the possibility of becoming comfortable with falling short. The possibility of becoming so used to failure that I stop fighting it. The possibility of hearing a warning and immediately assuming it is meant for someone else. The possibility of seeing my flaws clearly and still refusing to confront them.

I often think about how temporary everything around me is. Every conversation eventually ends. Every friendship changes. Every possession breaks. Every achievement fades. Every person leaves or is left behind. Time keeps moving regardless of whether I am making use of it or wasting it. Days become months. Months become years. Years become memories. The older I get, the faster it seems to happen. Things that felt far away suddenly feel close. Events I thought happened recently happened years ago. Dreams I once had are now buried under routine and responsibility.

Yet despite seeing how quickly life passes, I still act as though I have unlimited time. I still postpone things that matter. I still delay improvements that should have happened long ago. I still assume there will always be another chance waiting for me in the future. Maybe that is the greatest illusion of all. Not believing that life lasts forever, but behaving as though it does.

When I think about Jahannam, I do not think about a single mistake leading someone there. I think about patterns. Patterns of arrogance. Patterns of neglect. Patterns of selfishness. Patterns of ignoring truth. Patterns of repeatedly choosing comfort over growth. A person's destination is often the result of thousands of choices rather than one dramatic decision. And that thought forces me to look at my own patterns. What am I repeatedly choosing? What am I repeatedly delaying? What am I repeatedly ignoring? If my habits continue unchanged, where are they leading me?

These questions are uncomfortable, but perhaps they are supposed to be. Growth rarely begins with comfort. It begins with honesty. And honesty forces me to admit that I have spent too much time waiting for motivation. Waiting for the perfect moment. Waiting for a version of myself that suddenly becomes disciplined overnight. The reality is that person is never coming. Change does not arrive first. Action arrives first, and change follows afterward.

Maybe I have not completely lost my ability to listen. Maybe I have only buried it beneath excuses, distractions, and delays. Maybe the problem is not that I cannot hear the reminders. Maybe the problem is that I have become too skilled at avoiding their consequences. If that is true, then the solution is not learning something new. The solution is acting upon what I already know.

Because one day I will hear my final reminder. One day I will have my final opportunity to change. One day I will pray my last prayer, make my last decision, and live my last day. When that day comes, I do not want my greatest regret to be that I understood the truth but never truly followed it. I do not want to look back on a lifetime of intentions and realize that I spent more time planning to become better than actually becoming better.

For now, I am still here. I am still breathing. I am still being given chances I do not deserve. And perhaps that alone is a mercy. Perhaps the fact that I can recognize this struggle means the story is not finished yet. Perhaps there is still time, not to promise change tomorrow, but to begin it today.
